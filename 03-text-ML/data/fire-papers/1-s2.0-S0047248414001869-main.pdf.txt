The inﬂuence of climate change on human settlements in coastal areas is a central question for archaeologists. This paper addresses this issue by focusing on the Témara region in Morocco. The study
area was selected for two main reasons. First, it contains numerous caves with Upper Pleistocene deposits, which have yielded remains of anatomically modern humans in association with Aterian and
Iberomaurusian artifacts. Second, these caves are currently located on the shore, thus this region is
particularly sensitive to major climate change and sea level ﬂuctuations. Diachronic taphonomic study of
faunal remains from two sites in the region, El Harhoura 2 and El Mnasra caves, shows alternating human/non-human predator occupations. The lower layers of El Mnasra Cave dating to Oxygen Isotope
Stage (OIS) 5 have yielded diverse ungulate remains with signiﬁcant anthropogenic impact marks,
together with numerous mollusk shells, Nassarius shell beads, hearths, lithics, some bone tools and used
pigments. Faunal remains from the upper layers dating to OIS 4, 3 and 2 of El Harhoura 2 and El Mnasra
caves, largely dominated by gazelles, provide evidence of carnivore activities, such as tooth marks,
numerous semi-digested bones and coprolites alongside some anthropogenic signatures (cut marks and
burnt bones). Non-human predators appear to be the main agents responsible for faunal modiﬁcations
and accumulations. The ‘non-intensive’ nature of human occupation is conﬁrmed by analyses of the lithic
industry at El Harhoura 2. The ‘intensive’ human occupations date to OIS 5 and could have taken place
during wet periods in connection with high sea levels, which allowed the exploitation of shellﬁsh in this
area. ‘Non-intensive’ human occupations generally correspond to arid periods and lower sea levels,
during which the Témara area was further inland and may have been less attractive to humans.

discovery of an early anatomically modern human with archaic
features at Contrebandiers Cave, dating to 110e80 ka (e.g., Balter,
2011; Jacobs et al., 2011; Oujaa and Hublin, 2012), complements
previous remains found in the Témara caves and other parts of
Morocco for this period (e.g., Debénath, 1976, 1977e1978,
1979e1980, 1992, 2000; Ferembach, 1976, 1998; Roche, 1976;
Hublin, 1991, 2000; Smith et al., 2007).
In very rare cases, as at Taforalt, a non-Levallois ﬂake industry
tentatively attributed to the MSA has been recognized after the
Aterian. The industry recorded at Taforalt dates to around 25 ka
(OIS 3) and remains poorly deﬁned (e.g., Barton et al., 2005, 2013;
€dter et al., 2012).

The Iberomaurusian culture occurred in several North African
localities from approximately 20 to 10 ka (OIS 2 to the beginning of
the Holocene). These lithic industries are rich in microlithic tools,
primarily backed bladelets (e.g., Barton et al., 2005, 2007, 2013;
€dter et al., 2012; Sari, 2012, 2014).

Humans from this period have a completely modern anatomy. They
buried their dead, sometimes in large cemeteries and used artistic
representation (molding clay statuettes) as well as various types of
ornamentation (e.g., Hachi et al., 2002; Barton et al., 2005, 2007;
Hachi, 2006; Bouzouggar et al., 2008; Mariotti et al., 2009;
Belcastro et al., 2010; Humphrey et al., 2012; Aoudia-Chouarki, 2013).
Aterian and Iberomaurusian archaeological records have been
found in different areas of North Africa, such as in mountains (e.g.,
Nami, 2003; Aumassip, 2004; Bouzouggar et al., 2007; Nami and
Moser, 2010; Barton et al., 2013; Sari, 2014), in the desert (e.g.,
Cremaschi et al., 1998; Pasty, 1999; Aumassip, 2004; Garcea, 2004,
2012b) or on the coast where numerous sites have been identiﬁed
(e.g., Bouzouggar et al., 2002, 2010; Aumassip, 2004; El Hajraoui
et al., 2012b; Sari, 2014). This raises the question whether North
African Regions, currently located in coastal areas, were occupied

123

permanently. It also stresses the issue of the impact of sea level
shifts on these occupations. Initial insights on these issues have
been gained through the study of lithic industries and chronological
data in the Témara caves, located on the Atlantic coast near Rabat
(Morocco). These ﬁrst results have shown discontinuous human
occupations. According to optically stimulated luminescence (OSL)
dates, there are three episodes of MSA occupation, dating to
approximately 110e90 ka, 75 ka and 55 ka, in connection with the
wetter OIS 5c, 5a and OIS 3 periods separated by archaeologically
sterile layers (Jacobs et al., 2012b). In addition, the Iberomaurusian
culture is also present in the upper part of some sequences
(Nespoulet et al., 2008a).
In this paper we examine a number of questions. Were humans
the main agents responsible for faunal modiﬁcation/accumulation?
Did shifts of occupants occur in the caves over time? Were the
occupations identiﬁed by Jacobs et al. (2012b) always of similar
‘intensities’? Which factors inﬂuenced these changes of occupations? We use taphonomic studies to identify if predators accumulated and/or modiﬁed the large mammal faunal assemblages
throughout time in two caves in the Témara region, El Harhoura 2
(EH2) and El Mnasra (EM). Results are compared to past global
climatic shifts, local environmental records and sea level ﬂuctuations in order to understand the inﬂuence of these factors on the
occupation of the Témara caves by past human populations.
Témara region
Location and geological context
The Témara region, located south-west of Rabat, contains
several coastal caves (Doukkala 1 and 2 caves, Contrebandiers Cave,
El Mnasra Cave, El Harhoura 1 and 2 caves, Dar es Soltane 1 and 2
caves) (Fig. 1). The region is formed by a sequence of consolidating
dunes alternating with marine and continental deposits (Akil, 1985;
Chahid, 2011). The Témara caves were formed before OIS 5,
approximately 130 ka, by karstic and/or marine erosion of the
calcarenites (secondarily consolidated dune or ouljian palaeocliff).
They open onto a narrow coastal plain (‘Oulja’), which is protected
by the primary consolidated dune. This dune overlooks a platform
with rocks and water pools where the waves break (Fig. 2)
(Mhammdi et al., 2008). However, the landscape of the Témara
region has differed in the past. The slope rupture at the edge of the
continental platform is located between approximately 200
to 130 m on the Moroccan Atlantic coast. The continental platform extends to the Casablanca region (southewest of Témara)
over 49 km and into the Kénitra region (northeeast of Témara)
where it narrows, over 20 km (Joly, 1950; Cirac et al., 1979). Thus,
during periods of marine regression, the Témara caves could have
opened onto a continental plain, which may have extended for
several kilometers. During OIS 4 and 3, marine regression would
have moved the shoreline away from the caves, but certainly to a
lesser extent during OIS 3. During OIS 2 (the Last Glacial
Maximum), the shore was further from the caves.
The sedimentary record of the caves dates between approximately 120 and 6 ka BP (e.g., Jacobs et al., 2011, 2012a,b; Richter,
2012; Jacobs and Roberts, 2012a,b; Janati-Idrissi et al., 2012a,b).
These sequences cover the Upper Pleistocene and the Holocene,
and have recorded human and non-human occupations of the
caves in correlation with climate changes, therefore allowing us to
tackle the questions outlined above.

Background to El Harhoura 2 and El Mnasra caves
El Harhoura 2 Cave
El Harhoura 2 (EH2) Cave (33 570 08.900 N, 6 550 32.500 W) was
discovered in 1977 by André Debénath. Since 2001, this site has
been excavated by a Moroccan-French team (Nespoulet et al.,
2008a,b; Nespoulet and El Hajraoui, 2012b). The cave faces west
towards the ocean, around 300 m away from the current shoreline.
It is 18 m above sea level (Ref. Global Marine Level) and measures at
least 200 m2 (Nespoulet et al., 2008a). The main excavation work
has been undertaken in the cave entrance (‘entry area’) (Fig. 3). The
stratigraphy of the ‘entry area’ is composed of 11 main archaeostratigraphic layers. Layer 1 is attributed to the Neolithic (dated to
around 5800 BP by 14C) and historical periods (Daugas et al., 1999,
2008; Daugas, 2002; El Hajraoui and Nespoulet, 2002e2003). Layer
2 is attributed to the Iberomaurusian, while layers 3 to 11 are
attributed to the MSA. Layers 5, 7, 9, 10 and 11 are almost archaeologically sterile in lithic industries, or have very low artifact densities. It should be noted that layers 4B to 11 were only excavated
over a small surface (~2 m2) by a test-pit, and the bedrock has not
yet been reached (Fig. 3).
Optically stimulated luminescence measurements suggest dates
between 120 and 55 ka for the Upper Pleistocene deposits (layers

125

11 to 3) (Jacobs et al., 2012b; Jacobs and Roberts, 2012a, Fig. 3).
Combined electron spin resonance/UraniumeThorium (ESR-U/Th)
dating on fossil bovid teeth provide younger ages, between 92 ka
(layer 8) and 44 ka (layer 4A) (Janati-Idrissi et al., 2012a,b; Fig. 3).
Our study focused on faunal remains from layers 2, 3, 4A and
layer 8.
Layer 2 has yielded Iberomaurusian industries including blade
and bladelet production. Rare MSA artifacts reﬂect minimal mixing
with lower layers (Nespoulet et al., 2008a,b; Nespoulet and El
Hajraoui, 2012a). One burial has yielded a large and robust individual displaying dental avulsion of the ﬁrst upper incisors, which
is typical of the Iberomaurusian culture (Oujaa and Lacombe, 2012).
Layers 4A to 3 have yielded MSA industries, composed of
Levallois and micro-Levallois knapping tools, laminar artifacts,
side-scrapers and chopping tools. No tanged tools, characteristic of
the Aterian have been recovered despite an extensive excavated
surface. The low density of artifacts, small size of the products,
scarcity of cores, knapping preparation and maintenance, such as
resharpening by-products associated with a relatively high proportion of retouched tools may represent considerable group
mobility and short occupations (Nespoulet and El Hajraoui, 2012a).
Few mollusk shells have been collected (study in progress) and a
hearth is present in layer 4A.
Despite the small excavation area, layer 8 yielded a signiﬁcant
amount of lithic material in relatively pristine condition in comparison to artifacts from other layers (Nespoulet and El Hajraoui,
2012a). This layer also contained four Nassarius sp. (El Hajraoui
et al., 2012a) and a large number of marine mollusk shells, consisting mainly of limpets (Nouet et al., Submitted; Fig. 4).

El Mnasra Cave
El Mnasra (EM) Cave (33 550 40.900 N, 6 570 13.300 W) was discovered in 1956 by Jean Roche. In 2005, this cave was included in the
same archaeological program as EH2 Cave (Nespoulet et al.,
2008a,b; El Hajraoui et al., 2012c). El Mnasra Cave opens onto the
Atlantic Ocean towards the west, about 500 m from the shore. It is
14 m above the current sea level (Ref. Global Marine Level) and
measures approximately 400 m2 (Nespoulet et al., 2008a). Unlike

El Mnasra Cave shows a complex stratigraphy composed of 13
main archaeostratigraphical layers (Fig. 5). The central area of the
cave bedrock was reached in a test-pit (~4 m2). Layer 1 is a modern
disturbed soil, layer 2 contains Neolithic material and layer 3 has
been attributed in part to the Iberomaurusian. Layers 4 to 11 have
been ascribed to the MSA (Aterian). The basal deposits (layers 12
and 13) are archaeologically sterile (El Hajraoui and Debénath in
Nespoulet et al., 2008b; El Hajraoui and Debénath, 2012a). This
stratigraphy is currently under revision (Lenoble, 2010).
Optically stimulated luminescence measurements suggest dates
between 120 and 70 ka for the Upper Pleistocene deposits (layers
12 to 3). However, the date of 75 ka for layer 3 could correspond to
disturbances in the stratigraphy (Jacobs et al., 2012b; Jacobs and
Roberts, 2012b, Fig. 5). Combined ESR-U/Th dating on bovid fossil
teeth provided younger ages, between 89 and 67 ka for layers 6 to 7
(Janati-Idrissi et al., 2012a,b; Fig. 5).
Our study focused on faunal remains from a portion of layer 3
and layers 5, 6 and 7.

A middle portion of layer 3 (archaeostratigraphy of El Hajraoui
and Debénath in Nespoulet et al., 2008b), corresponding to unit 4
(lithostratigraphy of Lenoble, 2010), is only preserved at the bottom
of the cave (squares G13, F13, E13 and D13). This deposit consists of
brown-red sands suggesting continentalization and the aridiﬁcation of the environment. It has been tentatively attributed by geoarchaeologists to OIS 2 and/or OIS 3/4 (Lenoble, 2010).
The lithic industries from layers 5, 6 and 7 are characteristic of
the Aterian with the presence of tanged tools (Debénath and El
Hajraoui, 2012). One anthropogenic structure (layer 6), several
hearths (layers 5 and 6) (Nespoulet et al., 2008a,b; El Hajraoui et al.,
2012d), numerous Nassarius sp. (N > 200) and other mollusk shells,
such as limpet and mussel, pigments and bone tools are also present in these layers (El Hajraoui, 1994, b; Campmas, 2012a; El
Hajraoui et al., 2012a,e; El Hajraoui and Debénath, 2012b).
Several burrows (Lenoble, Study in progress) have cut through
all layers in the cave. Recent excavations in layer 3 (¼ unit 4) have
taken into account these disturbances by excluding remains from
the problematic areas. In layers 5, 6 and 7, large honey badger
(Mellivora capensis) burrows were observed during the excavation.
Also in layers 5, 6 and 7, an area of recent trampling has been noted,
which may be due to the intervention of a gardener or recent water
runoff leading to the collapse of archaeological sections in excavation squares H13, H14, G12 (Lenoble, Study in progress). The
material from this area has been excluded from our study
(Campmas, 2012a).

and are not discussed in this paper.

Bone surfaces were observed with low magniﬁcation using a
handheld lens (10). Several taphonomic processes such as
weathering, root etching or adhering matrix can alter the surface of
bones and hide marks left by the accumulating agents. These biases
were taken into consideration in our study. Some of the remains
with adhering matrix were treated with acetic acid to remove the
matrix. Therefore, poorly preserved bones (with important
adhering matrix, with important roots etching) were excluded from
the percentages of remains with marks.
Carnivore marks (tooth marks and semi-digested bones), cut
marks, percussion marks, and burnt bones were counted. More
details on the butchery sequences and the burnt bones will be
presented separately in a paper focusing speciﬁcally on human
activities and site functions for humans (Campmas, 2012a; Campmas et al., In progress).
The main criteria used by researchers for identifying agents
responsible for the faunal accumulations is the presence of tooth
marks (pits, grooves, scoring, depressions and notches) and
anthropogenic marks, such as cut marks and percussion marks with
the consideration of their location on long bones (e.g., Marean et al.,
2000; Assefa, 2006b; Costamagno et al., 2009; Thompson and
Henshilwood, 2011; Faith, 2013). The localization of carnivore and
anthropogenic marks on long bones as recommended by
Blumenschine (1988) were noted.
The frequencies of cut marks depends on many factors, such as
culinary preferences, butchery practices, numbers of hunted prey,
season, carcass size, butcher skill, tools used, post-depositional
fragmentation, surface preservation, magnifying glass used, count
type, excavation strategies, and choice of the analyst (e.g., Guilday
et al., 1962; Binford, 1978; Lyman, 1987, 1992; Blumenschine,
1988; Bartram, 1993; Fosse, 1994; Marean, 1998; Costamagno,
1999, 2012; Abe et al., 2002; Lupo and O'Connell, 2002; Egeland,
2003; Pobiner and Braun, 2005; Costamagno and David, 2009;
Dominguez-Rodrigo and Yravedra, 2009; Otarola-Castillo, 2010).
Due to this important variability of cut mark frequencies, the
dispersion of cut marks on long bones is not the best indicator of
the order of access to carcasses. Although the identiﬁcation of
notches has been performed according to the criteria of Capaldo
and Blumenschine (1994), allowing human hammerstone
breakage and carnivore tooth notches to be distinguished on the
basis of the morphology of notches on bovid long bones, several
notches have been considerated as ‘indeterminates’ due to the
difﬁculty of clearly distinguishing human and carnivore activities
and a low readability of medullar portions. Because cut mark frequencies are variable and notches made by human or carnivores are
difﬁcult to identify, we have focused our attention on the distribution of tooth marks. In addition, frequencies of tooth marks on

of complete remains as the length and circumference of the
conserved long bone shaft (Villa and Mahieu, 1991) and particularly
the length of shaft and middle shaft fragments.
Other elements, such as frequencies of semi-digested bones (e.g.,
Marra et al., 2004; Beauval and Morin, 2010; Discamps, 2011) and the
presence/abundance of coprolites (e.g., Cruz-Uribe, 1991; Pickering,
2002; Kuhn et al., 2010), give evidence of important carnivore or
other non-human predator presence in caves. Only partially digested
bones with a high degree of modiﬁcation were considered as digested, because light alterations could also be due to the acetic acid
treatment. The presence of coprolites was also considered, even if
coprolites were not systematically preserved or collected (especially
when broken). Carnivores generally destroy less dense parts of long
bones, such as epiphyses, to consume fat. This practice can produce
long bone shaft ‘cylinders’ (e.g., Binford, 1981; Brain, 1981; Haynes,
1983; Cruz-Uribe, 1991; Faith et al., 2007; Campmas and Beauval,
2008; Kuhn et al., 2010), thus the presence of long bone shaft ‘cylinders’ was also considered. The proportions of carnivores are a
supplementary index used to discuss carnivore occupations. Indeed,
rates of carnivore specimens are usually high in faunal accumulations

due to carnivore activities (e.g., Cruz-Uribe, 1991; Brugal et al., 1997;
Pickering, 2002; Kuhn et al., 2010). In addition, the presence of young
carnivores indicates the use of cave as maternity dens by carnivores
(e.g., Brugal et al., 1997; Fosse, 1997).

El Harhoura 2 Cave
The faunal spectrum is diverse with the presence of many ungulates and carnivores (Table 3). In layers 2, 3 and 4A, carnivores
represent between 10% and 16% of the NISP, and are dominated by
canids (such as jackal and fox) (Table 3 and Fig. 6). Large canids

Discussion
The presence of carnivore remains, carnivore marks, coprolites,
some cut marks, burnt bones, lithic artifacts, bone tools, used pigments and hearths points towards the intervention of multiple
agents, human and non-human predators, in these two caves. The
results are similar in the upper layers of EH2 Cave (layers 2, 3 and
4A) and EM Cave (layer 3 ¼ unit 4) and between the lower layers of
EM Cave (layers 5e7) and EH2 Cave (layer 8). Here, we examine the
main agents responsible for the accumulation and modiﬁcation of
these faunal assemblages, who occupied these caves, how they
varied throughout time, what factor(s) might have inﬂuenced the
change of occupants in the caves, and how EH2 and EM caves
compare to other sites in North Africa and elsewhere.

The North African fossil record has demonstrated the occurrence
of several carnivore species on the Atlantic coast of Morocco during
the Upper Pleistocene, which could have accumulated and modiﬁed
bone assemblages in the cave: small canids such as fox (Vulpes
vulpes) (e.g., Ruhlmann, 1951; Michel, 1990, 1992; Michel and
Wengler, 1993; Aouraghe, 2000, 2001; Monchot and Aouraghe,
2009; Michel et al., 2009, 2010; Bougariane et al., 2010; Daujeard
et al., 2011; Stoetzel et al., 2012; Amani et al., 2012a; Campmas,
2012a) and small jackal (Canis aureus and/or Lupulella sp.) (e.g.,
Michel, 1990, 1992; Michel and Wengler, 1993; Aouraghe, 2000,
2001; Monchot and Aouraghe, 2009; Michel et al., 2009, 2010;
Bougariane et al., 2010, 2012; Geraads, 2011; Stoetzel et al., 2012;
Campmas, 2012a; Amani et al., 2012b); large canids such as large
jackal (C. aureus or Canis sp.) (e.g., Michel, 1990, 1992; Aouraghe,
2000, 2001; Michel et al., 2009, 2010; Daujeard et al., 2011;
Geraads, 2011; Bougariane et al., 2012; Stoetzel et al., 2012;
Campmas, 2012a; Amani et al., 2012b); large felids such as lion
(Panthera leo) (e.g., Michel, 1990, 1992; Michel and Wengler, 1993;
Aouraghe, 2000, 2001; Monchot and Aouraghe, 2009; Daujeard
et al., 2011) and leopard (Panthera pardus) (e.g., Michel, 1990,
1992; Michel and Wengler, 1993; Aouraghe, 2000, 2001; Merzoug,
2005; Monchot and Aouraghe, 2009; Michel et al., 2009, 2010;
Bougariane et al., 2010; Daujeard et al., 2011; Stoetzel et al.,

2012); and hyenas such as spotted hyaena (Crocuta crocuta) (e.g.,
Michel, 1990, 1992; Michel and Wengler, 1993; Aouraghe, 2000,
2001; Monchot and Aouraghe, 2009; Michel et al., 2009, 2010;
Bougariane et al., 2010; Stoetzel et al., 2012) and striped hyena
(Hyaena hyaena) (e.g., Michel, 1990, 1992; Michel and Wengler,
1993; Aouraghe, 2000, 2001; Monchot and Aouraghe, 2009;
Daujeard et al., 2011). In addition, large raptors, such as bearded
vulture (Gypaetus barbatus) (e.g., Robert and Vigne, 2002a,b; Attard
and Reumer, 2009) or Egyptian vulture (Neophron percnopterus)
(Sanchis Serra et al., 2009, 2013), can also accumulate bones in nests
located in caves, and are still present today in Morocco (e.g.,
Génsbøl, 1993; Heinzel et al., 2004). A detailed discussion of the
carnivores and/or raptors that could be involved in accumulations is
beyond the scope of this article and is presented elsewhere
(Campmas, 2012a; Campmas et al., In progress).
In layers 2, 3 and 4A of EH2 Cave and in the studied part of layer
3 of EM Cave, carnivores represent between 10% and 16% of the
NISP, dominated by canids (such as jackal and fox). The carnivore
ratio is less than in European hyena dens (e.g., Brugal et al., 1997),
but is similar to fossil African carnivore dens and higher than in
present day hyena dens (e.g., Cruz-Uribe, 1991; Kuhn, 2011; Fourvel,
2012). Young carnivores are almost absent (remains from only one

European hyena dens (Brugal et al., 1997), but they are uncommon
in modern African hyena dens (e.g., Leakey et al., 1999; Lacruz and
Maude, 2005; Pokines and Kerbis-Peterhans, 2007; Egeland et al.,

Fossil records from North African contexts where agents
responsible for faunal accumulations have been identiﬁed can
provide clues for our study. At Gazelles Cave, where the number of
carnivore marks is low (17% of NISP and on 14% on shaft portions of
gazelles long bones), Daujeard et al. (2011) suggested that carnivores (mainly canids, and hyenids to a lesser extent) modiﬁed
faunal remains introduced naturally into the bottom of the cave
through the occulus. At EM Cave, the location of the excavation
under the oculus allows for this possibility, but at EH2 Cave there is
no oculus and the excavation is located at the entrance of the cave,
thus the function of the cavity as a trap can be excluded. The frequencies of carnivore marks at EH2 and EM caves are fewer than at
Feline Cave (30% NISP and 50% of shaft portion of long bones of
ungulates) (Daujeard et al., 2011) and Hominid Cave (20% NR 33% of
middle shaft portions) (Raynal et al., 2010, 2011; Daujeard et al.,
2012), which seems to correspond to carnivore dens. However,
the EM and EH2 caves faunal assemblages are highly similar to
those of El Harhoura 1 attributed to hyena dens (Monchot and
Aouraghe, 2009).
As distribution of tooth marks (pending the use of a method that
allows the removal of the adhering matrix efﬁciently while not
affecting bone surfaces) and fossil records have not allowed us to
identify the speciﬁc modifying/accumulating agents, it is necessary
to consider other elements. Semi-digested bones are numerous at
EH2 and EM caves. Frequencies at EM Cave are similar to fossil
European hyena cave dens (between 20% and 40%) (Beauval and
Morin, 2010). Fourvel (2012) in hyena caves found similar frequencies than at EH2 Cave, higher than in current African hyena
dens (mainly < 2%) where small fragments are not included in
studies. Thus, the non-readability of faunal remains provided by the
unplotted remains fraction could explain the small number of semidigested bones identiﬁed.
Semi-digested bones of ungulates are not produced only by
hyenas but also by large canids (Esteban-Nadal et al., 2010;
Esteban-Nadal, 2012; Fosse et al., 2012; Mallye et al., 2012) and
large raptors (e.g., Robert and Vigne, 2002a,b), indicating the possibility that several non-human predators intervened at Témara.
The presence of coprolites corroborates the identiﬁcation of
carnivore activity. The scarcity of coprolite fragments could be due
to their fragility as well as to the wet sieving and sorting during
excavations, because small coprolite fragments were not consistently collected. Recently, during the 2011 excavation, one of us
(Emilie Campmas) observed the presence of small coprolite fragments in sieve residues from layers 4b to 6 of EH2 Cave. It may be
necessary to resample the upper layers of EH2 Cave in order to
systematically evaluate the presence of small coprolite fragments in
sieve residues. Thus, on the basis of all of the evidence examined,
non-human predators seem to be main agent of modiﬁcation for
faunal remains at EH2 and EM caves.
Lower layers In layers 5e7 of EM, carnivore remains are rare,
particularly when intrusive honey badger remains are excluded.
Carnivore marks are again rare, while burnt bones are frequent,
particularly in connection with the numerous hearths. Despite the
low frequency of cut marks, such alteration is present on differentsized ungulates (Table 6) and could be explained by several factors:
1) Although most bones covered with adhering matrix have been
excluded from quantiﬁcation, remains with less adhering matrix
have been included. The presence of such adhering matrix
impedes the observation of osseous surfaces. 2) Burnt bones
(surface degradation and fragmentation) could decrease this rate.
3) Visualization with low magniﬁcation (x 10) could also limit the
observation of very thin cut marks. 4) Human butchery practices
can leave few marks. Indeed, although Dominguez-Rodrigo
(1997a,b) suggests that human accumulations are characterized
by high frequencies of cut marks, attaining up to 20% on humeri

and femora (long bones rich in meat), some butchery practices
such as boiling can explain the scarcity of cut marks (Costamagno
and David, 2009). However, several Middle Palaeolithic/Middle
Stone Age and Upper Palaeolithic/Later Stone Age assemblages,
that have been subject to taphonomic analysis and identiﬁed as
having been accumulated and primarily modiﬁed by humans,
have been noted to display highly variable frequencies of cut
marks (5e25% NISP) (Dominguez-Rodrigo and Yravedra, 2009). At
the Témara caves, the presence of cut marks, percussion marks,
burnt bones, mollusk shells, stone tools, bone tools, hearths, used
pigments and Nassarius sp. shell beads conﬁrms the human
occupation of these caves, which seems to be ‘intense’.
The characteristics of the archaeological material from layer 8 at
EH2 Cave are different from those in the upper layers. This layer
contains numerous mollusk shells, four Nassarius sp. and burnt
bones. Cut marks are present in low frequency, but are more
abundant than carnivore marks in the same layer, and cut marks in
other layers. In addition, carnivore numbers are low and numerous
size >1 ungulate remains are present. These results, associated with
other archaeological data for this layer, argue for more intensive
human occupations. New excavations and an extension of the testpit will allow us to conﬁrm this hypothesis.
Change of cave occupants throughout time
ESR-U/Th dates (Janati-Idrissi et al., 2012a,b) are younger than
dates obtained by OSL (Jacobs and Roberts, 2012a,b; Jacobs et al.,
2012b). The difference in age estimates between OSL and ESR
methods at present remains an unresolved problem. We chose to
base our reﬂection on OSL dates, because many sedimentary deposits from archaeological caves in the region of Temara (and other
regions in Morocco) have been dated by OSL, such as Dar es Soltane
1 (Barton et al., 2009; Schwenninger et al., 2010), Dar es Soltane 2
(Schwenninger et al., 2010), El Mnasra (Schwenninger et al., 2010;
Jacobs et al., 2012b; Jacobs and Roberts, 2012b), Contrebandiers
(Jacobs et al., 2011, 2012a) and El Harhoura 2 (Jacobs and Roberts,
2012a; Jacobs et al., 2012b). These dates present a high degree of
coherence between them, and with those obtained by thermoluminescence (TL) at Contrebandiers Cave (Jacobs et al., 2011, 2012b;
Richter, 2012). In addition, Jacobs et al. (2012b) have proposed that
human occupations have been correlated with wet periods, on the
basis of OSL dating and lithic industries.
According to the OSL method, deposition of layers 5e7 at EM
Cave and layer 8 at EH2 Cave, which have a higher anthropogenic
activity index than the other studied layers, date to OIS 5c (Jacobs
and Roberts, 2012a; Jacobs et al., 2012b). Layers 3 and 4A of EH2
Cave are attributed to OIS 4/3 (Jacobs et al., 2012b; Jacobs and
Roberts, 2012b). According to geoarchaeological observations
(Lenoble, In progress), a studied part of layer 3 of EM is tentatively
ascribed to OIS 2 and/or OIS 3/4. Non-human predators seem to be
the main occupants of the cave during these periods and human
occupation seems to be non-intensive (few anthropogenic marks
on faunal remains and lithic technology analyses; Nespoulet and El
Hajraoui, 2012a). Layer 2 of EH2 Cave has been attributed to OIS 2
based on the presence of lithic industries ascribed to the Iberomaurusian. Non-human predators also appear to be the main
agents for the modiﬁcation and/or accumulation of large faunal
remains, despite the change in the use of the cave by humans for
sepulchral activities, perhaps, integrated on a larger territory
crossed by Iberomaurusian groups.
The three periods of MSA human occupation identiﬁed by Jacobs
et al. (2012b), ~110e95 ka, ~80 ka and ~60 ka, do not seem to
display the same intensity of human occupation. During the ﬁrst
episode (OIS 5c; Jacobs et al., 2012b) the human occupation appears
more intensive. The third episode (OIS 3; Jacobs et al., 2012b)

E. Campmas et al. / Journal of Human Evolution 78 (2015) 122e143

corresponds to a low human intensity occupation associated with
non-human predator activities. Further taphonomic analyses of the
intermediate layers of EM and EH2 caves are required to explore
further the hypothesis of Jacobs et al. (2012b) and characterize the
second episode of MSA occupation (OIS 5a).
Correlation with local environmental changes
The Upper Pleistocene (OIS 5 to OIS 2) was subject to signiﬁcant
palaeoenvironmental change caused by glacial cycles and the
development of ice in the Northern Hemisphere. In North Africa,
the impact of these global phenomena corresponds to an alternation of arid and humid periods accompanied by landscape modiﬁcations, sea level ﬂuctuations and the movement of the shoreline
away from the studied coastal caves during several periods in the
past (e.g., Shackleton, 2000; Jouzel et al., 2002; Waelbroeck et al.,
2002) (Fig. 12). As humans intensively occupied these caves
approximately between 110 and 95 ka (e.g., Shackleton, 2000;
Jouzel et al., 2002; Waelbroeck et al., 2002, Fig. 12), Jacobs et al.
(2012b) suggested that these occupations were related to a wet
climate period. Here, we thus propose a model for the sequence of
carnivore/human occupations at Témara, taking into consideration
the inﬂuence of climate change and the date of the different
stratigraphic layers.

Currently, no archaeobotanical investigation has been carried
out at the Témara caves, and there are no Pleistocene sequences
suitable for pollen analyses in the Rabat-Témara-Casablanca region.
Continental pollen data are rare pre-40 ka in North Africa with most
pollen sequences located in Tunisia (e.g., Brun, 1989, 1991;
Stambouli-Essassi et al., 2007) and the Moroccan mountains (e.g.,
Ballouche, 1986; Lamb et al., 1988; Cheddadi et al., 1998; Ballouche
and Marinval, 2003; Rhoujjati et al., 2009). Other pollen sequences
are available for the Saharan area (e.g., Hooghiemstra and Agwu,
1988; Dupont and Hooghiemstra, 1989; Hooghiemstra et al.,
1992; Petit-Maire, 1992; Le Houérou, 1997; Hooghiemstra, 2004),
but far from non-desertic areas with Mediterranean climate inﬂuences. Thus, local palaeoenvironmental data dating to the Upper
Pleistocene from the Rabat-Témara area are based on faunal analysis, and especially on small vertebrates, which are better indicators of climatic and landscape changes in comparison to large
mammals (Stoetzel, 2009; Stoetzel et al., 2010, 2011, 2014).
At EH2 Cave, layer 8 has yielded microfauna mainly found in
open habitats, but also a good representation of taxa such as
amphibians (Bufo bufo, Pleurodeles cf. waltl) and some reptiles
(Natrix maura) from wet habitats (Stoetzel, 2009; Stoetzel et al.,
2010, 2011, 2014). Layer 6 of EM Cave has yielded remains
attributed to the rodent Arvicanthis sp. (Amani et al., 2012a;

they are clearly more numerous in layers 7e5 of EM Cave and at
the bottom of the layer 3 of EM Cave (corresponding to unit 6 of
the lithostratigraphy). Layer 8 of EH2 Cave has also yielded
abundant limpets (Nouet et al., In progress). During the period of
deposit of the layer 7e5 of EM, large ungulates, such as Bovini
and suids, are better represented. Their presence could correspond to the expansion of these species during wet periods and/
or increasing preference for them by humans (Campmas, 2012a).
Layers 4A and 3 at EH2 Cave, and perhaps a portion of the
studied part of the layer 3 of EM Cave, were deposited during OIS
4 and/or 3 (Jacobs et al., 2012b, Fig. 12). They correspond to nonhuman predator occupations and non-intensive human occupation. During these periods, sea levels ranged from 100 m (OIS 4)
and 70/-40 m (OIS 3) (e.g., Shackleton, 2000; Jouzel et al., 2002;
Waelbroeck et al., 2002). Consequently, the caves were located
further from the sea since the continental shelf is considerable in
this area (between 20 km and 50 km) (Joly, 1950). Thus, the caves
did not have the same attraction for MSA humans during this
period because they were not in the vicinity of the shore, despite
the presence of ungulates. The signiﬁcant number of gazelles
could correspond to increased aridity or preferences of nonhuman predators (Campmas, 2012a). However, microfaunal
studies of layers 3 and 4A of EH2 Cave denote a relatively wet and
open environment, close to that observed for the layer 8
(Stoetzel, 2009; Stoetzel et al., 2010, 2011, 2014). This result is in
agreement with the suggestion that there was a third MSA
occupation at EH2 Cave during the wet stage of the OIS 3 (Jacobs
et al., 2012b), but not with the same intensity than during OIS 5.
An alternative hypothesis suggests that the main activity of the
human occupation may have been located in another part of the
EH2 Cave.
Layer 2 of EH2 Cave, and perhaps a portion of the studied part of
the layer 3 of EM, were deposited during the Last Glacial Maximum
(OIS 2; ~30e10 ka) (Fig. 12). Palaeoecological data based on large
and small vertebrates indicate an aridiﬁcation and an opening of
the environment (Stoetzel et al., 2011, 2014; Stoetzel, 2013b). This
period also corresponds to the most signiﬁcant sea level regression
(120 m) (e.g., Shackleton, 2000; Jouzel et al., 2002; Waelbroeck
et al., 2002). Despite a signiﬁcant change in lithic industries (MSA
to Iberomaurusian) and the presence of a human burial (EH2, layer
2), the main agents of modiﬁcation/accumulation of large faunas
appear to be non-human predators (Campmas, 2012a,b). The
increasing proportion of large ungulates in layer 2 of EH2 could
have resulted from climatic inﬂuences on ungulate biomass
(however, the ﬁrst results from the studied part of the layer 3 of EM
Cave indicate that gazelle prevails). New dating of these layers will
enable us to deﬁne the exact timing of these deposits and/or
changes in non-human accumulators.

Comparison with other contexts
Comparison in the Rabat-Témara area Human occupation dates
during OIS 5 is also evident at Contrebandiers Cave (Jacobs et al.,
2011, 2012a), Dar es Soltane 1 (Ruhlmann, 1951; Barton et al.,
2009; Schwenninger et al., 2010), and Dar es Soltane 2
(Debénath, 1977e1978; Debénath et al., 1986; Schwenninger
et al., 2010). Several human occupation sites have yielded
Nassarius shell beads, such as El Mnasra, Contrebandiers and El
Harhoura 2 (e.g., El Hajraoui et al., 2012a). In contrast, carnivore
accumulation mainly identiﬁed without complete taphonomic
analyses, date mainly to OIS 4/3 as it is the case at Dar es Soltane
1 (Ruhlmann, 1951; Barton et al., 2009; Schwenninger et al.,
2010). At El Harhoura 1 Cave, taphonomic analyses have shown
that carnivores are the main accumulators of large fauna, but
without precise chronological context (Monchot and Aouraghe,
2009). These results are very similar to those obtained for upper
layers of EH2 Cave. The only exception that does not ﬁt our
model is the occupation from the top of Dar es Soltane 1 Cave
attributed to human occupation dating to OIS 3 and 4. However,
taphonomic analyses are required to evaluate the accuracy of
these interpretations, since human lithic artifacts are mixed with
non-human predator accumulations, as was observed at EH2 Cave
(layers 4A and 3).
When relative proportions of ungulate sizes are available, it
seems that humans are responsible for the accumulation of faunal
remains: larger ungulates are better represented, whereas gazelle
is predominant in non-human predator accumulations (Fig. 13). It is
notably the case at El Harhoura 1 and Doukkala 2 caves where
gazelles are abundant (Michel and Wengler, 1993; Monchot and
Aouraghe, 2009), and in the upper layers of EH2 and EM caves.
This observation may reﬂect prey selection by accumulators.
However, it is also possible that during the OIS 5 climate amelioration, environments were conducive to the development of large
ungulate herds, such as buffalo or aurochs, which were probably
generally associated with less arid environments (e.g., DespardEstes et al., 1991; Corson, 2002; van Vuure, 2002). Furthermore,
gazelles, which are both carnivore prey and human prey at EM and
EH2 caves, are present in other Témara caves, such as Dar es Soltane
2, El Harhoura 1, Contrebandiers and Doukkala 2 (Stoetzel et al.,
2014). To conclude, this change in ungulate size may be the
consequence of climate shifts or accumulator selection, or may be
both. These initial observations must be complemented by further
taphonomic studies, dating, and ﬁeld excavations.

In the Mediterranean area, at the site of Phacoche
Algeria), warthog and large Bovini dominate (Hadjouis, 1994,

In more inland sites in the Rif and eastern Morocco, at Taforalt,
the only layers bearing Nassarius sp. shell beads have been dated to

coastal area but also inland, is a key period for human evolution. In
addition, the presence of these marine shells (Nassarius sp.) further
inland argues for links/exchanges between coastal and inland
areas, perhaps thanks to the mobility of ancient hunter-gatherers
and/or contact among several groups.
Contexts other than North Africa Beyond Témara, Morocco and
North Africa in general, South African coastal sites show similar
shifts in occupation. For example at Ysterfontein 1 rock shelter,
despite the low chronological resolution, the uppermost layers
contain few artifacts or faunal remains. Tooth-marked bones and
occasional coprolites indicate that small carnivores and hyaenas
sometimes lived inside the shelter. The deepest layers contain
hearth lenses, numerous artifacts, and abundant intertidal shells
characteristic of MSA occupation (Avery et al., 2008). At Pinnacle
Point Cave 13B, the human occupation intensity has been
correlated to the distance to the shore. According to dates, more
intense human occupations (characterized by more lithic
industries, faunal remains, shellﬁsh and pigments) occurred
when the shore was near the cave (Marean, 2010).
As at Témara (Steele and Alvarez-Fernandez, 2011, 2012), OIS 5
(and OIS 6) coastal assemblages in southern Africa present higher
rates of mollusk consumption such as at Pinnacle Point Cave 13,
Klasies River, Blombos Cave, Ysterfontein 1 (Henshilwood et al.,
2001; Halkett et al., 2003; Marean et al., 2007; Avery et al., 2008;
Jerardino and Marean, 2010; Marean, 2010, 2011). In addition, the
presence of lithic industries on the Red Sea shore was attributed to
OIS 5, and may indicate the exploitation of shore resources by

(Walter et al., 2000; Bruggemann et al., 2004). In order to reﬁne
understanding of human adaptation to coastal environments, it is
necessary to identify precise changes in the shoreline position
through time (Fisher et al., 2010).
As is the case in North Africa during this period, in South Africa
ancient AMH used beads made from Nassarius sp. shells (d'Errico
et al., 2005; Marean et al., 2007; Marean, 2010, 2011). Also during
this time AMH ﬁrst appear in the Levantine area (e.g., Tillier, 2006)
where Nassarius shell beads have also been found (Vanhaeren et al.,
2006). Thus, the simultaneous emergence of all of these ‘modern’
behaviors in several distant geographic areas raises issues concerning exchanges and/or dispersal of AMH across the African
continent and the factors (climate, economic organization)
accompanying the emergence of these symbolic behaviors.
Anatomically modern humans are unlikely to have been
concentrated only in littoral areas or their surroundings, as there
are inland records, even in the central part of the Arabian Peninsula
(e.g., Groucutt and Petraglia, 2012; Boivin et al., 2013; Drake et al.,
2013) during OIS 5. It is suggested that human populations occupied coastal areas as refuges during arid periods (e.g., Mellars,
2006; Rose and Petraglia, 2009) though records for these periods
were submerged during sea level rises (e.g., Bailey and Flemming,
2008). However, archaeological evidence also suggests their presence during wetter periods.
We can ask the question of whether the exploitation of coastal
resources has played a major role for the emergence of Upper
Pleistocene innovations. Marean (2011: 434) suggests that “a
coastal niche provided excellent incubation conditions for the
material cultural expression of behavioral complexity, and may
explain the rather singular material cultural complexity [ … ]
during this crucial phase in the origins of modern humans”.
Erlandson (2001) discusses the fact that coastal adaptations facilitated larger group sizes and reduced mobility. “These larger group
sizes could add selective pressure on more effective mechanisms
for mediation of social relationships” (Marean, 2011: 435). Thus,
according to these ideas, littoral areas such as southern Africa and
Témara, besides being preferential areas for raw material collection
to make Nassarius shell beads, could have played a role for their use
as social symbolic hallmark. Future work on the exploitation of
coastal resources and coastal occupations in North Africa, especially
at Témara, will obviously reﬁne the understanding of the cultural
context in which particular Upper Pleistocene innovations
emerged, as it is the case for the production of Nassarius beads,
tracing symbolic behaviors, which could be local both in time and
in space (in speciﬁc regions with Mediterranean-type environment) (Cowling and Campbell, 1980; Mooney, 1988; Cowling et al.,
1996).
Conclusion
El Harhoura 2 and El Mnasra caves present long-term stratiﬁed
sequences, covering the whole Upper Pleistocene and Holocene
periods, allowing us to assess the timing of alternating human/nonhuman predator occupations, as well as the inﬂuence of climate
change. Taphonomic analyses of faunal remains result in the
identiﬁcation of the agents responsible for the accumulations and
large faunal modiﬁcations. In layers 2, 3 and 4A of EH2 Cave and the
studied part of layer 3 (¼ unit 4) of EM Cave, the taphonomic study
seems to indicate that non-human predators seem to be the main
agents for modiﬁcation/accumulation of large faunas, mainly made
up of gazelles. These results, associated with low densities of lithic
industries at EH2 Cave (Nespoulet and El Hajraoui, 2012a) show
that humans occupied the caves, but probably in a non-intensive
way. The presence of a burial in layer 2 of EH2 Cave evidences a

138

change of use of the cave by the Iberomaurusian populations, with a
sepulchral purpose.
At EM, humans are primarily responsible for the faunal accumulation in layers 5e7. Numerous in situ activities were practiced
on the site (butchery, use of ﬁre, stone tools, mollusk shells, Nassarius sp., and pigments) (Nespoulet et al., 2008a,b; Debénath and
El Hajraoui, 2012; El Hajraoui et al., 2012a,d,e), which conﬁrm
relatively intense human occupations. Even in layer 8 of EH2 Cave,
in which only a small surface has been excavated, there is a large
amount of evidence (such as cut marks more frequent than carnivore marks, burnt bones, numerous mollusk shells and four Nassarius sp.) for more intensive human occupation (Nespoulet et al.,
2008a,b; Campmas, 2012a; El Hajraoui et al., 2012a; Nespoulet
and El Hajraoui, 2012a; Nouet et al., In progress).
The location of the Témara area near the shore during periods of
high sea level could have played a role in these occupations. During
OIS 5c, humans occupied these caves intensively, certainly in correlation with high sea levels, perhaps in order to exploit shellﬁsh,
mainly mollusks (abundant in these layers). During periods of
climate degradation (OIS 4, 3 and 2), the caves are located further
inland and non-human predators seem to have been at the origin of
large fauna accumulations/modiﬁcations, mainly composed of
gazelles. Humans do not appear to have occupied the sites in an
intensive manner at this period. Throughout OIS 2, despite the
cultural change and sepulchral use of the EH2 cavity by Iberomaurusian populations (Oujaa and Lacombe, 2012), carnivores are
mainly responsible for the faunal accumulation.
The AMH/non-human predator relationships in cave occupations are complex. In spite of the limited extension of excavations in
several layers, the timing and causes of the alternating human/nonhuman predator occupations and the abandonment of caves by
humans have been clariﬁed at Témara, taking account of climate
oscillations. During OIS 5 in this area, as in other parts of Africa,
AMH exploited coastal environments and this period seems to have
played an important role for the dispersal of AMH. Future work on
intermediate periods between OIS 5 and the end of OIS 4, in other
parts of the caves and other faunal series, will continue to reﬁne
this global model of occupation.
